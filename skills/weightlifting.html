Weightlifting: Lifting Heavy Rocks

Eddie Yang | schedule 1-2 minutes

Weightlifting is the art of lifting heavy rocks.

In the winter of 11th grade, my friend dragged me into the gym. I had been, up until that point, in staunch opposition to keeping my body healthy, instead focusing mostly on school, video games, and playing instruments. I was extremely scrawny, and a little tubby at the same time, and really bad at pretty much every sport. Thankfully I wasn't made fun of in gym class (although funnily enough, I was on good terms with the jocks). Anyways, it's 11th grade, and my friend invited me to work out after school at our school's strength center. After a lot of convincing, I did my first workout. And I was hooked.

Since then I've done 5 workouts a week for several years, and that consistency has led to gains I'm proud of (though there's always higher to go on that ladder!). It's been a great way to de-stress and listen to music and also great for my body (and mind!).

The funny part is that having large muscles doesn't necessarily make you athletic, or should I say, doesn't make you good at every sport. I still suck at pretty much everything that requires practice, dexterity, or lots of endurance. I could maybe beat you in ping pong, though.

Hypertrophy

Hypertrophy is when you lift heavy rocks, and your muscles adapt and the cells (i.e. the muscle fibers themselves) grow larger in size.

Progressive Overload

The ancient Greeks figured out that if you lifted super heavy rocks, your muscles would get used to it, and you would be able to lift slightly heavier rocks the next time. Thus, the name is self-explanatory: we progressively overload our body by gradually increasing the intensity of our workouts so we'll always be challenged.
